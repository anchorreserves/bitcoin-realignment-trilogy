## Literature Review

The transmission mechanisms underlying Keynesian monetary policy rest upon assumptions that are intimately tied to the institutional features of fiat monetary systems. These frameworks, developed most influentially by Keynes (1936), Hicks (1937), and later formalized in modern treatments (Mankiw 2019), depend on liquidity preference as a controllable variable, the operation of fiscal and monetary multipliers, and the capacity for discretionary management of aggregate demand. Central to these approaches is the presumption of an elastic monetary base, fractional-reserve credit expansion, and uneven monetary distribution that enables authorities to influence real economic outcomes.

The Austrian perspective stands in clear contrast. It underscores the non-neutrality of money and the distortions produced by interventionist policies (Mises 1949; Rothbard 1963; Hayek 1976; Hülsmann 2008). The Fiat Stratification Feedback Model (Quinby 2026) supplied the first closed-loop account of how fractional-reserve lending, Cantillon effects, K-shaped divergence, and buy-borrow-die incentives interact to generate self-reinforcing wealth stratification. This third paper extends the analysis to its logical conclusion. It establishes that the very neutralizations identified in the Dismantlement Framework render the core transmission channels of Keynesian demand management structurally inoperable. The novelty of the trilogy therefore resides in this integrated before-and-after architecture: Keynesian interventionist tools function effectively within pure fiat environments but progressively lose efficacy as Bitcoin adoption reaches meaningful scale. This work thus offers a unified theoretical account of why certain long-standing policy frameworks may face fundamental obsolescence under an alternative monetary standard.

## 1. Liquidity Preference and the Inoperability of Monetary Multipliers under Bitcoin’s Bearer-Asset Constraint

Through its strict bearer-asset character and hardcoded supply of 21 million units, Bitcoin prevents commercial banks and custodians from expanding the money supply through credit creation. Unlike fiat deposits, Bitcoin exists exclusively as unspent transaction outputs controlled by private keys on an immutable public ledger. Any lending arrangement requires the outright transfer of private-key control. This architecture imposes a permanent protocol-level reserve ratio of zero percent by design rather than regulation. The system therefore moves from leverage-driven money creation to one grounded exclusively in prior savings and full-reserve arrangements. This shift directly disables the central engine that has historically enabled Keynesian liquidity preference to function as an adjustable policy variable and the associated monetary multipliers (Keynes 1936; Hicks 1937; Mankiw 2019).

Sound-money principles first clarify the mechanism. When discretionary control over money creation is removed, liquidity preference ceases to serve as a lever that central authorities can systematically offset through open-market operations or reserve adjustments (Mises 1949; Rothbard 1963; Hayek 1976). Holders maintain complete sovereignty over their units. Consequently, traditional multiplier effects become inoperable because the broad money supply cannot expand beyond the protocol-enforced ceiling. Monetary neutrality reasserts itself at the base layer: shifts in liquidity preference no longer generate amplified responses in aggregate demand.

### 1.1 Protocol Framework

The Bitcoin protocol enforces scarcity through a fixed supply cap and decentralized validation. Private-key custody ensures ownership cannot be replicated or diluted by intermediaries. No central bank or commercial institution possesses the ability to issue new units against existing holdings. Liquidity arises solely from actual holdings and secondary markets rather than through regulatory high-quality liquid asset buffers. In effect, the protocol eliminates the very distinctions that once allowed fiat institutions to operate with zero reserves while still expanding broad money.

Although the total supply remains capped at 21 million units, the monetary unit itself is infinitely divisible in practice. Each bitcoin consists of 100 million satoshis. This granularity allows the fixed base to scale across arbitrary levels of adoption and economic activity without violating the scarcity rule. The divisibility property thereby transforms the hard cap from a theoretical constraint into a functional medium of exchange. This structure severs the traditional link between liquidity preference and central-bank transmission mechanisms.

### 1.2 Shift from Credit Multiplication to Savings-Based Lending

When a holder deposits Bitcoin with a custodian or lending platform, the loaned units must leave the lender’s control entirely until repayment. No new bitcoin can be created to satisfy loan demand. Lending therefore originates strictly from prior savings. As Ammous (2018) demonstrates, this hard constraint forces a return to genuine savings-based lending and eliminates the artificial expansion of credit that characterizes fiat systems. This arrangement aligns with long-standing Austrian concerns regarding sound money while simultaneously eliminating the foundational channel through which Keynesian demand-management tools have operated. Liquidity preference, once a manipulable instrument of policy, reverts to a pure market phenomenon without any pathway to aggregate demand amplification.

### 1.3 Structural Implications

Capital allocation now rests with individual holders and transparent custodians rather than apex institutions that previously enjoyed temporal priority over newly created purchasing power. In interaction with Bitcoin’s fixed issuance schedule, the protocol eliminates the liquidity injections that once amplified Cantillon flows and enabled Keynesian stimulus transmission. Friedrich A. Hayek observed that we shall never have sound money again unless we take it from the hands of government, not through violence but by some slick roundabout way (Hayek 1976). Bitcoin’s bearer-asset design and protocol-enforced scarcity constitute precisely that roundabout path. Holders now stand outside the banking pyramid entirely, their private keys serving as the only gatekeeper between personal wealth and any external claim. Federal Reserve Distributional Financial Accounts reports (2021–2025) highlight the contrast with fiat systems, where broad-money expansion through credit creation remains possible. On-chain analytics from Glassnode and Chainalysis confirm that Bitcoin lending protocols operate exclusively on a full-reserve basis, with loaned units fully transferred rather than multiplied (Glassnode 2024; Chainalysis 2025). Mainstream Keynesian analyses continue to emphasize interest-rate policy and forward guidance (Mankiw 2019; Krugman 2013). Yet these instruments presuppose an elastic monetary base that Bitcoin’s protocol does not provide. Acknowledged limitations include transitional hybrid regimes in which fiat infrastructure continues to operate alongside growing Bitcoin self-custody, as well as current data constraints regarding long-term scalability.

## 2. Neutralization of Discretionary Stimulus Transmission via Proof-of-Work Issuance

Bitcoin’s decentralized issuance through proof-of-work and its immutable, predictable schedule eliminate the temporal priority that defines the Cantillon Effect under fiat systems. New units enter the network solely through competitive block validation, with every participant subject to identical computational rules and no privileged entry point. The protocol’s halving schedule, occurring every 210,000 blocks, and the absolute 21-million-unit cap stand fixed and publicly verifiable in advance. This structure removes the sequencing advantage that once enabled Keynesian stimulus to flow first through financial institutions and asset markets, thereby disabling the primary transmission channel for discretionary monetary expansion.

Sound-money principles clarify the mechanism. Monetary injections are never neutral; they confer immediate real gains on early recipients before prices adjust (Mises 1949; Rothbard 1963; Hayek 1976). When issuance occurs through open competition rather than central-bank open-market operations or quantitative easing, the Cantillon sequencing channel closes entirely. No institution holds temporal priority over new supply. Consequently, discretionary stimulus loses its structural pathway: policymakers can no longer direct new purchasing power toward targeted sectors to stabilize aggregate demand or exploit Phillips-curve trade-offs.

### 2.1 Protocol Issuance Framework

The Bitcoin protocol enforces a hardcoded, predictable issuance schedule through proof-of-work consensus. Block rewards constitute the sole source of new supply, and the difficulty adjustment maintains a fixed average rate independent of demand fluctuations. This mechanism stands in direct contrast to the discretionary tools of open-market operations, quantitative easing, and reserve adjustments that underpin Keynesian stimulus transmission in fiat systems. Full nodes, operated independently by any participant, validate every block and enforce the same consensus rules, ensuring that even large mining pools cannot unilaterally alter issuance.

### 2.2 Removal of Temporal Priority and Loss of Aggregate Demand Stabilization

Because new units enter the system through open competition rather than privileged channels, no first-mover advantage exists for any institution. The absence of temporal priority severs the Cantillon sequencing that previously allowed stimulus to reach financial institutions and asset markets ahead of wage earners. As a result, Keynesian aggregate demand stabilization loses its structural foundation: discretionary monetary expansions can no longer generate the uneven purchasing-power effects that amplify multipliers and sustain Phillips-curve assumptions. The loss of sequencing advantage registers first in policy terms, yet a vivid contrast brings the physical reality of Cantillon severance into sharper relief.

These properties undermine the Phillips-curve assumptions of a stable inflation-unemployment trade-off by removing the ability to erode real wages through discretionary monetary expansion (Keynes 1936; Mankiw 2019). The Bitcoin Dismantlement Framework (Quinby 2026) demonstrates how this protocol-level shift alters long-term economic trajectories.

Early access to credit once enabled asset accumulation and further borrowing capacity primarily for the upper cohort. Bitcoin changes this dynamic. ## 4. Realignment of Tax Incentives and the Neutralization of Debt-Financed Demand Management

The realignment of tax incentives under Bitcoin fundamentally weakens the buy-borrow-die strategy that has long reinforced wealth stratification and debt recycling into the banking system. With no ability to expand the money supply through credit creation and no temporal priority in issuance, the structural advantages that once made leveraged asset retention highly attractive diminish significantly. This change neutralizes an important transmission channel for Keynesian fiscal policy, which has historically relied on debt-financed demand stimulus.

### 4.1 Protocol and Tax Incentive Framework

Bitcoin’s bearer-asset design grants individuals full control over their holdings through private keys. This self-custody model stands in direct contrast to the custodial relationships that underpin traditional buy-borrow-die strategies in fiat systems. Because new units cannot be created to collateralize additional debt, and because appreciation occurs within a fixed supply, the tax code’s preference for borrowing against unrealized gains loses much of its previous power.

### 4.2 Erosion of Buy-Borrow-Die Strategies

The strategy of buying assets, borrowing against them to avoid capital gains taxes, and passing them on with a step-up in basis at death becomes far less compelling when the underlying money is hard. As Antonopoulos (2017) has famously observed, Bitcoin fundamentally changes the nature of ownership and control: “Your keys, your coins. Not your keys, not your coins.” This principle of true self-custody reduces reliance on intermediaries and perpetual debt structures. Holders gain the ability to exercise direct sovereignty over their capital, diminishing both the incentive and structural necessity for the leveraged tax-arbitrage game that previously recycled liquidity back into the fractional-reserve system.

### 4.3 Implications for Fiscal Multipliers and Demand Management

Debt-financed government spending loses effectiveness when the private sector can no longer easily expand credit in response and when asset holders have less reason to engage in perpetual borrowing. The recycling mechanism that once amplified fiscal multipliers through the banking system weakens considerably. Bitcoin’s pseudonymity and enforcement challenges accelerate this neutralization: because holdings are controlled solely by private keys and transactions occur on a pseudonymous ledger, governments confront insurmountable practical difficulties in tracking, valuing, and taxing Bitcoin assets at scale. Jurisdictional competition compounds the effect. Capital and individuals can migrate across borders with negligible friction to friendlier regulatory environments, limiting any single government’s ability to impose the very debt-based incentives that have sustained Keynesian demand management. Bitcoin’s fixed supply and bearer-asset design render deficit monetization impossible, thereby constraining the capacity of any authority to shift real resource claims onto future generations through inflation. These protocol features do not constitute transitional limitations. Rather, they represent permanent structural barriers that render fiscal intervention progressively inoperable as adoption thresholds are reached. As a result, traditional Keynesian tools for managing aggregate demand through deficit spending face irreversible headwinds under widespread Bitcoin use.

## 6. Counterarguments and Acknowledged Limitations

The structural obsolescence claim advanced in this paper must be weighed against several important counterarguments and practical realities. Critics may point to Bitcoin’s continued volatility, slow global adoption rates, potential regulatory suppression, scalability constraints, and the likely persistence of hybrid monetary regimes for decades. This section addresses these concerns directly while preserving the distinction between transitional frictions and the long-term structural disablement of Keynesian transmission mechanisms.

### 6.1 Volatility and Adoption Thresholds

One common objection centers on Bitcoin’s price volatility, which some argue precludes it from functioning as a viable monetary standard capable of displacing fiat systems. Volatility at present levels largely reflects Bitcoin’s position as a nascent asset class undergoing price discovery. Glassnode data through 2025 show Bitcoin’s annualized volatility has declined from peaks above 80 percent in its early years to roughly 40–50 percent amid growing institutional participation and deeper liquidity pools (Glassnode 2024; Chainalysis 2025). As adoption broadens and liquidity deepens, historical patterns of monetary regime shifts suggest that such volatility tends to moderate over time. The protocol’s fixed supply and predictable issuance schedule nevertheless continue to exert structural pressure on Keynesian policy tools regardless of short-term price fluctuations.

### 6.2 Regulatory Resistance and Jurisdictional Dynamics

Skeptics contend that governments will successfully restrict or prohibit Bitcoin through regulation, taxation, or outright bans. Yet the protocol’s bearer-asset nature, pseudonymity, and global permissionless distribution create significant enforcement challenges. El Salvador’s 2021 adoption of Bitcoin as legal tender, followed by similar explorations in Argentina and several African nations, illustrates how jurisdictional competition already constrains regulatory reach. Capital and individuals can relocate to more favorable environments simply by memorizing a seed phrase, effectively carrying their wealth as portable knowledge. This mental portability stands in sharp contrast to the physical seizure risks inherent in gold or other traditional monetary instruments. These dynamics reinforce rather than undermine the obsolescence argument.

### 6.3 Data Limitations and Hybrid Regime Persistence

Bitcoin’s history as a monetary technology spans less than two decades, and current on-chain metrics indicate that self-custodied Bitcoin still represents only a small fraction of global wealth. Transitional hybrid regimes, in which fiat systems continue to coexist with growing Bitcoin usage, will therefore likely persist for an extended period. These realities represent important practical limitations and deserve explicit acknowledgment. However, they do not alter the core mechanism-level conclusion: once Bitcoin adoption crosses critical thresholds, the transmission channels that have sustained Keynesian demand management for nearly a century become structurally inoperable.

Most contributions remain either purely theoretical or limited to isolated empirical episodes. Full closed-loop models that link specific mechanisms to protocol-level neutralizations and supply executable falsification protocols are exceptional. The closest parallels arise in landmark regime-shift analyses such as Friedman and Schwartz’s Monetary History or Hayek’s Denationalisation of Money, where theoretical architecture meets sustained empirical pressure. The present framework therefore belongs to a thin but established tradition of mechanism-driven regime comparisons rather than the more common narrow exchanges.
