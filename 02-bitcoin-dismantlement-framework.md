## Abstract

This paper introduces the Bitcoin Dismantlement Framework as a direct theoretical counterpoint to the Fiat Stratification Feedback Model (Quinby 2026). Where the original model integrates fractional reserve lending, the Cantillon Effect, K-shaped economic divergence, and tax incentives for debt-financed asset retention (buy-borrow-die) into self-reinforcing dual feedback loops that amplify wealth stratification, the present framework demonstrates how Bitcoin’s protocol systematically dismantles each mechanism.

Bitcoin’s fixed supply of 21 million units, decentralized issuance through proof-of-work, and bearer-asset design prevent ex nihilo credit creation, neutralize uneven monetary distribution, mitigate structural polarization, and realign tax and debt incentives toward market-driven outcomes. Grounded in Austrian principles of sound money and monetary non-neutrality, the analysis draws on empirical observations from on-chain analytics, adoption patterns in high-inflation economies, and Bitcoin-based lending protocols. The framework shows that these protocol properties enforce monetary discipline and individual sovereignty, thereby fostering a flatter financial hierarchy through voluntary exchange rather than institutional privilege.

Transitional challenges, including volatility, scalability constraints, regulatory uncertainty, and early-adopter concentration, receive explicit consideration, along with acknowledged limitations in long-term empirical data. The paper positions the Bitcoin Dismantlement Framework as an evidence-based extension of the original model, highlighting mechanisms that restore free-market dynamics while preserving scholarly neutrality.

## Introduction

The Fiat Stratification Feedback Model (Quinby 2026) integrates four mechanisms within contemporary fiat monetary and fiscal systems to explain self-reinforcing wealth stratification. Fractional reserve lending creates new money through credit expansion. The Cantillon Effect distributes this purchasing power unevenly, privileging early recipients. K-shaped economic divergence produces sustained upward trajectories for asset-rich households alongside stagnation or decline for wage-dependent ones. Tax incentives for debt-financed asset retention, known as buy-borrow-die strategies, reinforce asset concentration and recycle debt into the banking system.

Together these elements generate a positive feedback loop that concentrates liquidity and opportunity at the upper economic strata (for example, the top 1 percent) and a parallel negative feedback loop that erodes stability at the lower end, with the middle class pulled apart in the central K-structure. The model observes that these dynamics persisted under the pre-2020 10 percent reserve regime and intensified after the March 2020 elimination of reserve requirements in conjunction with quantitative easing, yet remain endogenous amplifiers rather than exclusive drivers of polarization.

Readers will benefit from consulting the antecedent Fiat Stratification Feedback Model (Quinby 2026) prior to engaging with the present framework, as the analysis proceeds in deliberate parallel structure and references specific mechanisms, sections, and diagrams from that work.

The analysis remains grounded in Austrian principles of sound money and monetary non-neutrality (Mises 1949; Rothbard 1963; Hayek 1976) while incorporating verifiable empirical observations and acknowledging transitional limitations.

## Literature Review

The four core mechanisms examined in the Fiat Stratification Feedback Model draw from longstanding economic ideas, though their unification into a closed-loop system remained uncommon until 2026 (Quinby 2026). Historical contexts such as the post-World War II era of monetary expansion occasionally compressed inequality, reminding us that these effects can vary cyclically. Richard Cantillon (1755) pioneered the insight that new money injects into the economy unevenly. This notion evolved through Mises (1949), Rothbard (1963), and Hülsmann (2008), forming the Austrian view of money’s non-neutrality. Analyses after 2008 and particularly after March 2020 showed how zero-reserve policies and quantitative easing intensified these distributions. Empirical studies on K-shaped divergence (Stiglitz 2020; Chetty et al. 2020) and tax-driven strategies (Saez and Zucman 2019; Batchelder and Kamin 2022) further illuminated the components. Prior work stopped short of the unified dual-feedback synthesis offered in the 2026 model.

While individual Austrian critiques of fiat credit expansion and observations on Bitcoin’s fixed-supply properties appear in existing literature, the present paper offers a distinct contribution. It constructs the Bitcoin Dismantlement Framework as the first systematic counter-model that directly parallels and dismantles each mechanism of the Fiat Stratification Feedback Model. The novelty resides in this explicit parallelism, the integration of protocol-level properties with the original feedback loops, and the visual inversion of the K-structure into convergent merit-based dynamics. No prior work has positioned Bitcoin’s architecture as a direct theoretical sequel that simultaneously breaks both the positive and negative loops, thereby creating an immediate research dialogue between the two frameworks.

Bitcoin’s decentralized protocol structure and realigned incentive system provide the foundation for this dismantlement. Through proof-of-work consensus and a fixed supply cap of 21 million units enforced by distributed agreement, the protocol removes discretionary monetary intervention. Participants earn rewards solely for honest validation of the ledger rather than for proximity to credit creation. The bearer-asset design further enables true self-custody, granting individuals permanent control over their holdings and an immediate exit option from any intermediary. These features shift economic behavior away from rent-seeking through fiat privilege toward productive effort, long-term planning, and voluntary exchange. Incentive realignment is central to the framework. It systematically undermines the mechanisms that sustain the dual feedback loops in the original model.

## 1. Bitcoin’s Bearer Asset Nature and the Constraints on Fractional Reserve Lending

Through its bearer-asset character and strict cap of 21 million units, Bitcoin prevents commercial banks or custodians from creating new units via credit issuance. Unlike fiat deposits, Bitcoin exists solely as unspent transaction outputs controlled by private keys on a transparent, immutable blockchain. Lending requires the complete transfer of private-key control to the borrower or custodian; this structure leaves no room for claims that exceed actual holdings. The March 2020 removal of fiat reserve requirements amplified credit expansion in the original model, yet Bitcoin’s protocol imposes a permanent 0 percent effective reserve ratio by design, not regulation. Under Bitcoin’s rules constraint shifts the system from leverage-based money creation to genuine savings or full-reserve arrangements, directly dismantling the engine that initiated the positive feedback loop in the Fiat Stratification Feedback Model.

### 1.1 Protocol Framework

The Bitcoin protocol enforces scarcity through a hardcoded supply cap and decentralized validation. Private-key custody ensures that ownership cannot be replicated or diluted by intermediaries. No central authority or commercial bank can issue additional units against deposits. Liquidity is maintained solely through actual holdings or secondary markets, not through high-quality liquid asset buffers calibrated to stress tests. In practice, the protocol eliminates the regulatory distinctions that once permitted fiat institutions to function with zero reserves while still expanding the broad money supply.

Bitcoin’s supply cap is enforced at the protocol level, yet the unit of account itself is infinitely divisible in practice. Each bitcoin comprises 100 million satoshis, the smallest indivisible subunit recognized by the network. This eight-decimal-place granularity ensures that even as total issuance approaches 21 million units, the monetary base can accommodate arbitrary levels of adoption and transaction volume without requiring any change to the hard-coded scarcity rule. The divisibility property therefore converts the fixed supply from a theoretical limit into a scalable medium that supports voluntary exchange at every economic stratum.

### 1.2 Shift from Credit Multiplication to Savings-Based Lending

A deposit of Bitcoin with a custodian or lending platform becomes the exact basis for any loan, with no multiplier effect. The loaned units leave the lender’s control entirely until repayment. New Bitcoin cannot be created to meet loan demand. This structure forces lending to originate from prior savings rather than simultaneous deposit creation, aligning with Austrian concerns about the ethics of fractional reserves (Rothbard 1963). Empirical examples from Bitcoin-based DeFi protocols and custody services illustrate lending volumes constrained strictly by available on-chain supply.

### 1.3 Structural Implications

Capital allocation now rests with individual holders and transparent custodians rather than apex institutions that previously enjoyed temporal priority over newly created purchasing power. In interaction with Bitcoin’s fixed issuance schedule, the protocol eliminates the liquidity injections that amplified Cantillon flows in the original model. Federal Reserve data on post-2020 M2 growth contrast sharply with Bitcoin’s predictable issuance, underscoring how the bearer-asset design removes the structural advantage previously granted to originating banks and their counterparties.

## 2. Neutralizing the Cantillon Effect through Decentralized and Predictable Issuance

The Cantillon Effect describes the non-neutral impact of new money creation, whereby early recipients gain real resources before general price levels adjust. The protocol achieves neutralization of the Cantillon mechanism by means of fixed, predictable issuance and a truly global, permissionless distribution process. New units enter the system solely via proof-of-work mining, with no central gatekeeper or privileged access point. Bitcoin removes the temporal priority that amplified uneven distribution in the Fiat Stratification Feedback Model.

### 2.1 Proof-of-Work, Halving Schedule, and Global Distribution Mechanics

Bitcoin issues new units at a predetermined schedule that halves approximately every four years and will reach zero around the year 2140. Fresh units enter the network exclusively through proof-of-work mining, a process open to anyone worldwide under identical rules with the same low barrier to entry simply by running open-source software and expending computational resources. Full nodes, operated independently by any participant, validate all blocks and enforce the same consensus rules, ensuring that even large mining pools cannot unilaterally alter the protocol. Miners compete globally to solve cryptographic puzzles, and the successful block adds exactly the scheduled reward to the miner’s address on the public ledger. No institution or government can accelerate, redirect, or withhold issuance. This process distributes new Bitcoin based solely on contributed work, eliminating the fiat pyramid in which the Federal Reserve and primary dealers occupy the apex.

### 2.2 Removal of Early-Recipient Privilege and Asymmetric Purchasing-Power Transfer

With the supply schedule established and known in advance, any rise in fiat M2 simply lifts Bitcoin’s exchange rate in uniform fashion across every holder, irrespective of acquisition timing. Late recipients experience no relative loss of purchasing power compared with early holders. The protocol enforces simultaneous and proportional adjustment across the entire monetary base, removing the silent transfer from late to early recipients that defined the Cantillon Effect in the original model. This uniformity prevents the asymmetric purchasing-power transfer described in the 2026 paper.

### 2.3 Comparative Analysis with Fiat Amplification Post-2020

In the zero-reserve environment after March 2020, repeated credit expansions magnified Cantillon flows by directing liquidity first to asset markets and connected institutions. Bitcoin’s design prevents any such sequencing. On-chain data confirm that halvings have consistently produced predictable issuance reductions without creating first-receiver advantages. Federal Reserve DFA reports on post-2020 M2 spikes contrast sharply with Bitcoin’s transparent, non-discretionary supply curve.

## 3. Bitcoin and the Mitigation of K-Shaped Economic Polarization

K-shaped economic divergence became evident after the 2020 crisis. Asset-rich households experienced sustained growth while wage-dependent ones stagnated or declined. Bitcoin counters this dynamic through its resistance to monetary dilution and the accessibility of self-custody tools.

### 3.1 Visualization and Definition

Plotted against time, asset-rich entities and households previously traced the upper arm of the “K,” while wage-dependent and small-business households traced the lower arm. The divergence appeared structural under fiat conditions. Bitcoin alters that trajectory. Inflation-resistant savings and low-cost transfer networks enable broader participation. The middle class converges upward rather than splitting apart.

### 3.2 Empirical Drivers

Early access to credit once enabled asset accumulation and further borrowing capacity for the upper cohort alone. Bitcoin changes the equation. Its fixed supply preserves real wages for the lower cohort, while tools such as the Lightning Network facilitate micro-transactions and remittances with minimal fees. Small businesses and wage earners gain the ability to form capital without reliance on distorted credit terms or rising input costs. Adoption patterns in high-inflation economies illustrate this shift. Households in Argentina and El Salvador have used Bitcoin to protect purchasing power and conduct borderless payments, narrowing the gap that widened under fiat systems after 2020 (Chainalysis 2023–2025). These observations remain suggestive at this stage, however, given the limited time horizon and potential confounding macroeconomic variables.

### 3.3 Long-Term Polarization

The shape once became self-perpetuating over time. Bitcoin reverses the process. The diverging trajectories give way to convergence as the positive reinforcement loop extends beyond the top strata and the negative loop loses its force. Countervailing factors receive further detail in Section 5.3. The opposing effects that once concentrated advantage at the top and eroded position at the bottom now neutralize each other. ## 4. Bitcoin and the Realignment of Tax Incentives

Modern tax regimes (United States and peer jurisdictions) contain structural features that preferentially reward debt-financed asset retention over asset liquidation or earned-income accumulation. In the Fiat Stratification Feedback Model, these incentives functioned as the stabilizing glue that locked in the dual self-reinforcing feedback loops (Quinby 2026). The bearer-asset nature of Bitcoin, together with the practical demands of self-custody and the fixed supply of 21 million units, alters the underlying economics of collateralization, debt servicing, and realization events.

### 4.1 Debt-Is-Not-Income Rule in a Bearer-Asset Context

Proceeds from loans remain excluded from taxable income, whereas the sale of appreciated assets triggers capital-gains recognition. Bitcoin held under self-custody cannot easily serve as collateral for conventional low-interest bank loans without the holder surrendering private-key control. This requirement raises both the operational complexity and the effective risk of leveraging Bitcoin holdings compared with traditional assets supported by fractional-reserve liquidity. The protocol therefore shifts the cost-benefit calculation away from perpetual debt rollover toward either outright sale or higher-cost specialized lending platforms.

### 4.2 Interest Deductibility and the Limits of Rollover Strategies

Interest expense on investment-related debt is frequently deductible. Bitcoin lending markets, whether centralized or decentralized, typically require substantial over-collateralization (often 150 percent or more) due to price volatility and operate at market-determined interest rates materially higher than those available for fiat collateral. Consequently, the protocol tilts the cost-benefit balance away from endless debt rollover and toward either outright sale or reliance on higher-cost specialized lending platforms. These conditions weaken the incentive to perpetually roll debt against unrealized gains (the buy-borrow-die pathway). The fixed supply of 21 million units, divisible to 100 million satoshis per bitcoin, further alters the calculus by providing a predictable scarcity premium that can make clean realization more attractive in certain scenarios relative to leverage. The step-up in basis at death remains applicable, but the overall tax-distortion advantage narrows.

### 4.3 Severed Feedback into the Monetary System

Repaid or rolled loans in the fiat system re-entered the banking system as new deposits, supplying raw material for further credit creation. Bitcoin lending transfers existing units without creating new ones. Repayment or liquidation therefore moves only existing units and severs the closed loop that once linked tax incentives to further fractional-reserve multiplication in the original model.

Empirical observations from on-chain analytics and lending protocols (for example, Aave, Compound, and institutional custody services) indicate that Bitcoin-collateralized lending exists but remains constrained by market terms significantly different from fiat strategies. Mainstream analyses note ongoing challenges including price volatility and evolving tax and regulatory clarity (Saez and Zucman 2019). These factors constitute acknowledged transitional frictions in the realignment process.

### 4.4 Bearer Mobility and Jurisdictional Competition

Bitcoin’s bearer-asset design enables instantaneous, low-cost cross-border transfer of value without intermediary approval or geographic restriction. This portability reduces the extent to which any single jurisdiction can treat resident holdings as captive for tax purposes. Early empirical patterns in El Salvador, where Bitcoin was granted legal-tender status in 2021, illustrate the mechanism: observed capital inflows and subsequent adjustments in tax and residency policy reflect competitive pressures on the domestic tax base (Chainalysis 2023–2025 reports).

In the context of the original model, this dynamic further erodes the closed-loop feedback between tax incentives and fractional-reserve credit expansion by exposing jurisdictions to potential outflow of mobile capital. Mainstream analyses note countervailing regulatory responses in other jurisdictions (e.g., enhanced reporting requirements and potential capital controls). Data on the long-run fiscal impact remain limited, as the phenomenon is still in its early stages.

### 4.5 Pseudonymity and Enforcement Challenges

The pseudonymous character of Bitcoin ownership, combined with true self-custody via private keys, introduces practical enforcement challenges for tax authorities. Unlike fiat assets held through regulated intermediaries, Bitcoin holdings generate no automatic third-party reporting, and private-key control prevents unilateral seizure or liens without the holder’s cooperation. This reduces the enforceability of capital-gains realization events and collateral-based strategies that previously reinforced perpetual debt rollover. Austrian analyses of tax minimalism underscore how such voluntary compliance regimes shift power toward individual monetary sovereignty (Rothbard 1973). Mainstream responses, including expanded broker reporting rules and international information-exchange agreements, represent ongoing attempts to restore visibility, yet the bearer nature of Bitcoin limits their effectiveness at scale. On-chain data and adoption patterns indicate that self-custody rates continue to rise (Chainalysis 2023–2025). Consequently, the closed-loop linkage between tax distortions and fractional-reserve credit expansion that stabilized the original model is further eroded. Data on the long-run fiscal impact remain limited, as the phenomenon is still in its early stages.

## 5. Integration: The Bitcoin Realignment Framework

Taken together, the preceding sections illustrate how Bitcoin’s protocol properties individually constrain or neutralize each of the four mechanisms that sustained wealth stratification in the antecedent model. When examined in combination, these properties interact to break both self-reinforcing feedback loops simultaneously. The fixed supply and bearer-asset design eliminates ex nihilo credit creation. Predictable proof-of-work issuance removes temporal priority in monetary distribution. Inflation resistance and accessible self-custody tools reverse structural polarization. Self-custody requirements and jurisdictional mobility weaken the tax distortions that previously locked the loops in place. The result is not merely the absence of stratification amplifiers but the emergence of a unified positive reinforcement cycle grounded in productivity, voluntary exchange, and sound money.

### 5.1 Dismantlement of the Positive Self-Reinforcing Loop (Upper Arm of the K)

In the original model the positive loop concentrated advantage among asset-rich households through leverage, early access to new credit, and tax-enabled debt rollover (Quinby 2026, Section 5.1). At its core, the protocol blocks concentration of advantage by requiring actual transfer of holdings or market-rate over-collateralized loans. Because no new units can be created against deposits, borrowing requires the actual transfer of existing holdings or over-collateralized market-rate loans. The absence of Cantillon sequencing means new purchasing power cannot be captured disproportionately by institutions with privileged access. Tax incentives no longer recycle debt into further credit expansion. Consequently, the upper-arm advantage that once compounded through institutional privilege gives way to competition based on productive contribution and accurate price signals.

### 5.2 Dismantlement of the Negative Self-Reinforcing Loop (Lower Arm of the K)

The negative loop in the original model eroded position for wage-dependent households through diluted real wages, constrained credit access, and rising input costs (Quinby 2026, Section 5.2). Bitcoin’s fixed supply preserves the purchasing power of earned income over time. Self-custody and low-cost transfer tools (for example, the Lightning Network) enable capital formation without reliance on traditional intermediaries. Adoption patterns in high-inflation economies illustrate the mechanism: households previously trapped in the lower arm have used Bitcoin to maintain real savings and conduct borderless transactions, narrowing the divergence observed after 2020 (Chetty et al. 2020; Chainalysis reports 2023–2025). The negative feedback dynamic therefore loses its structural fuel.

### 5.3 Emergence of Unified Reinforcement Across Strata

Once both opposing loops lose their structural fuel, the familiar K-shaped divergence gives way to convergence across economic strata. In this environment, productivity gains together with the gentle deflationary pressure of a fixed supply steadily raise real wealth for every participant, regardless of starting position. As the network expands, rising security and value in turn encourage broader participation and higher productivity. This single positive reinforcement cycle operates uniformly across lower, middle, and upper strata, replacing institutional privilege with merit-based outcomes. These conditions promote more precise capital allocation. Without the crutch of cheap credit, businesses face genuine market discipline. Companies producing inferior or less-desired products exit the market more quickly. This strengthens overall competition and reduces the misallocation of scarce resources. Empirical observations remain early-stage, and mainstream analyses continue to highlight transitional volatility and scalability questions (Krugman 2013). Nevertheless, the protocol-level mechanics establish a theoretical pathway toward flatter financial hierarchy through voluntary exchange.

### 5.4 Game-Theoretic Dynamics of Network Adoption

Bitcoin's fixed-supply protocol also creates game-theoretic incentives that strengthen this unified reinforcement cycle. Each participant faces a coordination problem in which individual choices depend on the expected actions of others. Early adoption takes place through voluntary calculation of Bitcoin's properties as a form of sound money (Rothbard 1962; Hayek 1976). As institutional participation and certain nation-state experiments grow, the relative cost of remaining fully exposed to fiat assets rises for those who stay outside of the Bitcoin network. Reports from Chainalysis covering 2023 to 2025 document this pattern of gradual uptake followed by faster expansion once a critical threshold is crossed. The process is consistent with Austrian accounts of spontaneous monetary order (Menger 1892; Hayek 1976). At the same time, researchers from other traditions point to regulatory barriers and short-term price volatility as factors that can delay or modify this trajectory. Long-term data on these coordination effects are still developing.
